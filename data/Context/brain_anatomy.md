# Brain Anatomy: Comprehensive Reference for MRI Analysis

## Overview of Brain Organization

The human brain is organized into several major divisions:
- Cerebral hemispheres (telencephalon)
- Diencephalon (thalamus, hypothalamus)
- Brainstem (midbrain, pons, medulla)
- Cerebellum

Total brain volume: ~1200-1400 cm³ in adults
Gray matter: ~600-700 cm³
White matter: ~500-600 cm³

## Cerebral Hemispheres

### Major Lobes

The cerebral hemisphere is divided into five lobes:

#### 1. Frontal Lobe
**Location**: Anterior to central sulcus, superior to lateral (Sylvian) fissure

**Major gyri**:
- Precentral gyrus: Primary motor cortex (Brodmann area 4)
- Superior frontal gyrus
- Middle frontal gyrus
- Inferior frontal gyrus: Contains Broca's area (areas 44, 45)

**Key sulci**:
- Central sulcus: Separates frontal from parietal lobe
- Precentral sulcus
- Superior and inferior frontal sulci

**Functions**:
- Motor control (precentral gyrus)
- Motor planning (premotor cortex, supplementary motor area)
- Executive function (dorsolateral prefrontal cortex)
- Language production (Broca's area)
- Personality and behavior (orbitofrontal cortex)
- Working memory

**Volume**: ~200-220 cm³ per hemisphere

#### 2. Parietal Lobe
**Location**: Posterior to central sulcus, superior to lateral fissure, anterior to parieto-occipital sulcus

**Major gyri**:
- Postcentral gyrus: Primary somatosensory cortex (areas 1, 2, 3)
- Superior parietal lobule
- Inferior parietal lobule:
  - Supramarginal gyrus (area 40)
  - Angular gyrus (area 39)

**Key sulci**:
- Postcentral sulcus
- Intraparietal sulcus
- Parieto-occipital sulcus

**Functions**:
- Somatosensory processing (postcentral gyrus)
- Spatial awareness and attention
- Language processing (inferior parietal lobule)
- Visuospatial integration
- Mathematical cognition (angular gyrus)

**Volume**: ~120-140 cm³ per hemisphere

#### 3. Temporal Lobe
**Location**: Inferior to lateral fissure, anterior to occipital lobe

**Major gyri**:
- Superior temporal gyrus: Contains primary auditory cortex (areas 41, 42)
  - Posterior portion: Wernicke's area (area 22)
- Middle temporal gyrus
- Inferior temporal gyrus
- Fusiform gyrus: Face recognition area
- Parahippocampal gyrus

**Key sulci**:
- Superior temporal sulcus
- Inferior temporal sulcus
- Collateral sulcus

**Medial structures**:
- Hippocampus: Memory formation
- Amygdala: Emotion processing
- Entorhinal cortex: Memory gateway

**Functions**:
- Auditory processing
- Language comprehension (Wernicke's area)
- Memory formation (hippocampus)
- Object recognition
- Emotion (amygdala)
- Visual processing (ventral stream)

**Volume**: ~110-130 cm³ per hemisphere

#### 4. Occipital Lobe
**Location**: Posterior to parieto-occipital sulcus and pre-occipital notch

**Major gyri**:
- Cuneus (medial surface)
- Lingual gyrus (medial surface)
- Lateral occipital gyri

**Key sulci**:
- Calcarine sulcus: Contains primary visual cortex (area 17/V1)
- Parieto-occipital sulcus

**Functions**:
- Primary visual processing (V1)
- Higher visual processing (V2-V5)
- Color vision
- Motion detection

**Volume**: ~60-80 cm³ per hemisphere

#### 5. Limbic Lobe (Insular Cortex)
**Location**: Deep to lateral fissure (insula), plus medial limbic structures

**Components**:
- Insula: Deep to frontal, parietal, and temporal opercula
- Cingulate gyrus: Medial surface, superior to corpus callosum
- Parahippocampal gyrus

**Functions**:
- Emotion processing
- Interoception (insula)
- Pain perception
- Autonomic control
- Motivation and reward (anterior cingulate)

### Important Anatomical Landmarks

**Sulci and Fissures**:
1. **Central sulcus**: Separates frontal from parietal lobe; runs from superior border to lateral fissure
2. **Lateral (Sylvian) fissure**: Separates temporal lobe from frontal and parietal lobes
3. **Parieto-occipital sulcus**: Separates parietal from occipital lobe (medial surface)
4. **Calcarine sulcus**: Contains primary visual cortex
5. **Cingulate sulcus**: Runs parallel to corpus callosum on medial surface

**Opercula**:
- Frontal operculum: Covers anterior insula
- Parietal operculum: Covers superior insula
- Temporal operculum: Covers inferior insula

## Functional Neuroanatomy

### Motor System

#### Primary Motor Cortex (Brodmann Area 4)
- **Location**: Precentral gyrus
- **Somatotopic organization**: Motor homunculus
  - Medial surface: Lower limb
  - Lateral convexity: Upper limb
  - Lateral inferior: Face
- **Disproportionate representation**: Face and hands have larger cortical areas
- **Output**: Corticospinal tract (pyramidal tract)

#### Supplementary Motor Area (SMA)
- **Location**: Medial superior frontal gyrus, anterior to primary motor cortex
- **Functions**:
  - Planning complex movements
  - Coordination of bimanual actions
  - Internal movement generation
  - Action sequences
- **Clinical**: Lesions cause contralateral weakness and speech disruption

#### Premotor Cortex
- **Location**: Lateral frontal lobe, anterior to primary motor cortex
- **Functions**:
  - Externally guided movements
  - Visual-motor integration
  - Mirror neurons (understanding others' actions)
- **Subdivisions**: Dorsal and ventral premotor areas

### Sensory System

#### Primary Somatosensory Cortex (Brodmann Areas 1, 2, 3)
- **Location**: Postcentral gyrus
- **Somatotopic organization**: Sensory homunculus (parallel to motor)
- **Modalities**:
  - Tactile sensation
  - Proprioception
  - Pain and temperature (via spinothalamic pathways)
- **Area 3a**: Proprioception from muscles
- **Area 3b**: Touch from skin
- **Area 1**: Texture
- **Area 2**: Size and shape

#### Secondary Somatosensory Areas
- **Location**: Parietal operculum
- **Functions**: Complex tactile processing, bilateral integration

### Language System

The brain employs two parallel processing streams for language:

#### Dorsal Stream (Phonological Processing)
- **Pathway**: Superior temporal gyrus → Supramarginal gyrus → Inferior frontal gyrus
- **White matter**: Superior longitudinal fasciculus/arcuate fasciculus
- **Function**: Sound-to-motor mappings for speech production and articulation
- **Lateralization**: Predominantly left hemisphere
- **Tasks**: Speech repetition, phonological processing

#### Ventral Stream (Lexical-Semantic Processing)
- **Pathway**: Middle/inferior temporal gyri → Ventrolateral prefrontal cortex
- **White matter**: Inferior occipitofrontal fasciculus
- **Function**: Sound-to-meaning mappings for comprehension
- **Lateralization**: Bilateral (less lateralized than dorsal stream)
- **Tasks**: Semantic access, language comprehension

#### Language-Critical Areas

**Broca's Area (Inferior Frontal Gyrus)**:
- **Location**: Pars opercularis and pars triangularis (areas 44, 45)
- **Traditional function**: Expressive language production
- **Modern understanding**: Internal conflict resolution, selecting among competing linguistic representations
- **Lesion effects**: Broca's aphasia (non-fluent, effortful speech)

**Wernicke's Area**:
- **Traditional location**: Posterior superior temporal gyrus (area 22)
- **Caveat**: Recent research questions exact localization; lesions often extend beyond classical boundaries
- **Function**: Receptive language, auditory comprehension
- **Lesion effects**: Wernicke's aphasia (fluent but meaningless speech)

**Posterior Inferior Parietal Lobule**:
- **Angular gyrus**: Critical for reading comprehension, semantic processing
- **Supramarginal gyrus**: Phonologic processing, speech repetition

### Visual System

#### Primary Visual Cortex (V1, Brodmann Area 17)
- **Location**: Banks of calcarine sulcus
- **Retinotopic organization**: Foveal representation at posterior pole
- **Function**: Initial cortical processing of visual information

#### Visual Association Areas
- **V2, V3**: Surrounding V1, intermediate visual processing
- **V4**: Color processing
- **V5/MT**: Motion detection

#### Ventral Visual Stream ("What" pathway)
- **Route**: Occipital → Temporal cortex
- **Function**: Object recognition
- **Key areas**:
  - Fusiform face area: Facial recognition
  - Visual word form area: Reading
  - Parahippocampal place area: Scene recognition

#### Dorsal Visual Stream ("Where" pathway)
- **Route**: Occipital → Parietal cortex
- **Function**: Spatial processing, visuomotor integration

## Deep Brain Structures

### Basal Ganglia

The basal ganglia are a cluster of subcortical nuclei deep within the cerebral hemispheres.

#### Core Components

**Striatum (Largest component, ~10 cm³)**:
1. **Dorsal striatum**:
   - **Caudate nucleus**: C-shaped structure
     - Head: Lateral to anterior horn of lateral ventricle
     - Body: Along lateral ventricle
     - Tail: Curves into temporal lobe
   - **Putamen**: Lateral to globus pallidus
   - **Function**: Conscious motor movements, executive functions

2. **Ventral striatum**:
   - Nucleus accumbens
   - Olfactory tubercle
   - **Function**: Reward and aversion responses

**Lenticular Nuclei** (lentiform nucleus):
- **Putamen**: Lateral component
- **Globus pallidus externus (GPe)**: Medial external segment
- **Globus pallidus internus (GPi)**: Medial internal segment

**Substantia Nigra**:
- **Location**: Midbrain
- **Pars compacta (SNc)**: Dopaminergic neurons
- **Pars reticulata (SNr)**: Output nucleus

**Subthalamic Nucleus (STN)**:
- **Location**: Diencephalon, inferior to thalamus
- **Shape**: Biconvex lens-shaped
- **Function**: Key relay in indirect pathway

#### Basal Ganglia Circuitry

**Direct Pathway** (facilitates movement):
- Cortex → Striatum → GPi/SNr → Thalamus (VA/VL) → Cortex
- Effect: Disinhibits thalamus, facilitating movement
- Neurotransmitters: Dopamine (D1 receptors) excites this pathway

**Indirect Pathway** (inhibits movement):
- Cortex → Striatum → GPe → STN → GPi/SNr → Thalamus → Cortex
- Effect: Inhibits thalamus, suppressing unwanted movements
- Neurotransmitters: Dopamine (D2 receptors) inhibits this pathway

**Hyperdirect Pathway** (rapid inhibition):
- Cortex → STN → GPi/SNr → Thalamus → Cortex
- Effect: Rapid inhibitory control, shorter conduction time
- Function: Emergency brake on movement

#### Striatal Microarchitecture
- **Medium spiny neurons (MSNs)**: 90% of striatal neurons
  - Direct pathway MSNs: Express D1 receptors, substance P
  - Indirect pathway MSNs: Express D2 receptors, enkephalin
- **Interneurons**:
  - Cholinergic interneurons: Modulate MSN activity
  - GABAergic interneurons: Local inhibition

**Compartmentalization**:
- **Striosomes (patches)**: Receive limbic cortical input
- **Matrix**: Receives motor and sensory cortical input
- Functional significance: Different cortical-basal ganglia loops

#### Connectivity Patterns
- **Cortical input**: ~5000 glutamatergic afferents per MSN
- **Convergence**: ~100 MSNs project onto each pallidal neuron
- **Dopaminergic innervation**: One dopaminergic neuron makes up to 1 million synaptic contacts
- **Thalamic input**: Virtually all striatal neurons receive thalamostriatal afferents

#### Functional Domains
1. **Motor domain**: Posterior putamen, sensorimotor cortex loops
2. **Associative domain**: Caudate, anterior putamen, prefrontal loops
3. **Limbic domain**: Ventral striatum, orbitofrontal and cingulate loops

#### Blood Supply
- **Lenticulostriate arteries**: Branches of middle cerebral artery
- **Clinical**: Prone to hemorrhage in hypertension ("stroke arteries")

#### Clinical Correlations

**Parkinson's Disease**:
- Pathology: Degeneration of SNc dopaminergic neurons
- Mechanism: Reduced dopamine shifts balance toward indirect pathway
- Result: Excessive STN-GPi activity, reduced thalamocortical facilitation
- Symptoms: Bradykinesia, rigidity, tremor

**Huntington's Disease**:
- Pathology: CAG repeat mutation, MSN degeneration
- Mechanism: Preferential loss of indirect pathway neurons initially
- Result: Reduced inhibition, excessive movement
- Symptoms: Chorea, cognitive decline

**Hemiballismus**:
- Pathology: STN lesion (often stroke)
- Mechanism: Loss of STN excitation of GPi
- Result: Disinhibited movement
- Symptoms: Violent flinging movements of contralateral limbs

**Dyskinesias**:
- Pathology: Abnormal D1 receptor activation in striatum
- Mechanism: Excessive direct pathway activity
- Common cause: Levodopa treatment in Parkinson's disease

### Thalamus

The thalamus is the major relay station for sensory and motor information.

#### Major Nuclei Groups

**Anterior Nuclear Group**:
- **Components**: Anteroventral (AV), anteromedial (AM), anterodorsal (AD)
- **Connections**: Mammillary bodies → anterior nuclei → cingulate cortex
- **Function**: Part of Papez circuit for memory
- **Blood supply**: Polar artery

**Medial Nuclear Group**:
- **Mediodorsal (MD) nucleus**:
  - Magnocellular: Connects to orbitofrontal cortex
  - Parvocellular: Connects to dorsolateral prefrontal cortex
  - Function: Executive function, working memory, emotion

**Lateral Nuclear Group**:
1. **Ventral tier**:
   - **Ventral anterior (VA)**: Basal ganglia output → premotor cortex
   - **Ventral lateral (VL)**: Cerebellum and basal ganglia → motor cortex
   - **Ventral posterior (VP)**:
     - VPL (ventral posterolateral): Body sensation
     - VPM (ventral posteromedial): Face sensation

2. **Lateral tier**:
   - **Lateral dorsal (LD)**: Parietal cortex connections
   - **Lateral posterior (LP)**: Parietal and occipital connections

**Posterior Nuclear Group**:
- **Pulvinar**: Largest thalamic nucleus
  - Visual attention
  - Visuospatial processing
  - Connections: Occipital, parietal, temporal cortices
- **Medial geniculate nucleus (MGN)**: Auditory relay
- **Lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN)**: Visual relay

**Midline and Intralaminar Nuclei**:
- **Centromedian (CM)**: Projects to striatum
- **Parafascicular (Pf)**: Projects to striatum
- **Intralaminar nuclei**: Arousal and attention
- **Midline nuclei**: Limbic connections

#### Thalamic Connectivity
- **Reciprocal connections**: Nearly all thalamocortical projections have corticothalamic feedback
- **First-order nuclei**: Relay peripheral information (VPL, VPM, LGN, MGN)
- **Higher-order nuclei**: Relay cortical information (pulvinar, MD, LP)

#### Blood Supply
- **Polar artery**: Anterior nuclei
- **Paramedian arteries**: Medial nuclei, intralaminar
- **Inferolateral arteries**: Ventral nuclei (VPL, VPM)
- **Posterior choroidal arteries**: Pulvinar, lateral geniculate

### Hypothalamus

#### Location and Boundaries
- **Ventral**: Forms floor and lateral walls of third ventricle
- **Superior boundary**: Hypothalamic sulcus
- **Anterior boundary**: Lamina terminalis
- **Posterior boundary**: Posterior perforated substance

#### Nuclei Organization (Anterior to Posterior)

**Anterior Region**:
- Supraoptic nucleus: ADH production
- Paraventricular nucleus: Oxytocin, ADH, CRH
- Suprachiasmatic nucleus: Circadian rhythm (master clock)
- Anterior nucleus: Thermoregulation (heat dissipation)

**Tuberal Region**:
- Arcuate nucleus: Growth hormone releasing hormone, dopamine
- Ventromedial nucleus: Satiety center
- Dorsomedial nucleus: Blood pressure, feeding
- Lateral nucleus: Hunger center

**Posterior Region**:
- Posterior nucleus: Thermoregulation (heat conservation)
- Mammillary bodies: Memory (Papez circuit)

#### Functions
- Autonomic regulation
- Endocrine control (via pituitary)
- Temperature regulation
- Circadian rhythms
- Feeding and satiety
- Emotional expression
- Memory (mammillary bodies)

#### Pituitary Connection
- **Infundibulum**: Connects hypothalamus to pituitary
- **Median eminence**: Neurosecretory zone
- **Hypophyseal portal system**: Transports releasing hormones

### Hippocampus and Medial Temporal Lobe

#### Hippocampal Formation

**Components**:
1. **Dentate gyrus**: Input region
2. **Hippocampus proper** (Cornu Ammonis):
   - CA1: Output to subiculum
   - CA2: Small region
   - CA3: Recurrent connections
   - CA4: Within dentate gyrus
3. **Subiculum**: Output region

**Location**: Medial temporal lobe, curves along floor of inferior horn of lateral ventricle

**Trisynaptic Circuit**:
1. Entorhinal cortex → Dentate gyrus (perforant pathway)
2. Dentate gyrus → CA3 (mossy fibers)
3. CA3 → CA1 (Schaffer collaterals)

**Function**:
- Declarative memory formation
- Spatial navigation (place cells)
- Pattern separation (dentate gyrus)
- Pattern completion (CA3)

#### Entorhinal Cortex
- **Location**: Anterior parahippocampal gyrus
- **Function**: Gateway to hippocampus
- **Grid cells**: Spatial navigation
- **Early Alzheimer's pathology**: Neurofibrillary tangles

#### Amygdala
- **Location**: Anterior to hippocampus in temporal lobe
- **Nuclear groups**:
  - Basolateral: Inputs from sensory cortices
  - Centromedial: Outputs to hypothalamus, brainstem
  - Cortical: Olfactory processing
- **Functions**:
  - Fear conditioning
  - Emotional memory
  - Threat detection
  - Social behavior

## White Matter Tracts

### Corpus Callosum
- **Largest white matter structure**: ~200 million fibers
- **Segments**:
  - Rostrum: Connects orbital frontal cortices
  - Genu: Connects prefrontal cortices
  - Body: Connects motor and sensory cortices
  - Splenium: Connects parietal, temporal, occipital cortices

### Association Fibers (Intrahemispheric)

**Short Association Fibers** (U-fibers):
- Connect adjacent gyri
- Course beneath sulci

**Long Association Fibers**:

1. **Superior Longitudinal Fasciculus (SLF)** / **Arcuate Fasciculus**:
   - Connects frontal, parietal, temporal regions
   - Subdivisions: SLF I, II, III, and arcuate
   - Function: Phonological processing, language production

2. **Inferior Longitudinal Fasciculus (ILF)**:
   - Connects occipital and temporal lobes
   - Function: Visual object recognition

3. **Inferior Occipitofrontal Fasciculus (IFOF)**:
   - Connects occipital, parietal, temporal to frontal lobe
   - Function: Semantic processing, reading

4. **Uncinate Fasciculus**:
   - Connects anterior temporal to orbitofrontal cortex
   - Function: Emotion, memory, language

5. **Cingulum Bundle**:
   - Runs within cingulate gyrus
   - Function: Limbic connections, emotion, memory

### Projection Fibers (Corticosubcortical)

**Descending**:

1. **Corticospinal Tract** (Pyramidal Tract):
   - Origin: Primary motor cortex, premotor, supplementary motor
   - Course: Corona radiata → Internal capsule (posterior limb) → Cerebral peduncle → Pyramids → Decussation → Lateral corticospinal tract
   - Function: Voluntary movement
   - Somatotopy: Face lateral, leg medial in internal capsule

2. **Corticobulbar Tract**:
   - Origin: Motor cortex
   - Termination: Cranial nerve nuclei in brainstem
   - Function: Facial, tongue, pharyngeal, laryngeal movements

**Ascending**:

1. **Thalamocortical Radiations**:
   - **Anterior thalamic radiation**: Anterior/medial nuclei → frontal cortex
   - **Superior thalamic radiation**: VA/VL → motor cortex
   - **Posterior thalamic radiation**: VPL/VPM → sensory cortex
   - **Inferior thalamic radiation**: Pulvinar, MG, LG → temporal, parietal, occipital

2. **Optic Radiations** (Geniculocalcarine Tract):
   - Origin: Lateral geniculate nucleus
   - Course:
     - Upper fibers (superior retina): Parietal lobe → superior bank of calcarine
     - Lower fibers (inferior retina, Meyer's loop): Temporal lobe → inferior bank of calcarine
   - Function: Vision
   - Clinical: Meyer's loop lesions cause contralateral superior quadrantanopia

### Internal Capsule

Critical white matter structure containing most projection fibers.

**Segments**:
1. **Anterior limb**: Between caudate and lentiform
   - Frontopontine fibers
   - Anterior thalamic radiation
2. **Genu**:
   - Corticobulbar fibers
3. **Posterior limb**: Between thalamus and lentiform
   - Corticospinal tract (somatotopic: face anterior, leg posterior)
   - Sensory radiations (VPL, VPM → cortex)
4. **Retrolenticular**: Posterior to lentiform
   - Optic radiation
   - Parieto-occipital connections
5. **Sublenticular**: Inferior to lentiform
   - Auditory radiation
   - Meyer's loop (inferior optic radiation)

**Blood Supply**: Lenticulostriate arteries (anterior circulation)

**Clinical**: Lacunar infarcts in internal capsule cause pure motor or sensory strokes

## Ventricles and CSF Spaces

### Ventricular System

**Lateral Ventricles** (paired):
- **Frontal horn**: Frontal lobe
- **Body**: Frontal and parietal lobes
- **Atrium** (trigone): Junction of body, occipital, temporal horns
- **Occipital horn**: Occipital lobe
- **Temporal horn** (inferior horn): Temporal lobe
- Volume: ~7-10 mL each

**Third Ventricle**:
- **Location**: Between thalami
- **Walls**: Hypothalamus (lateral), thalamus (dorsal)
- **Recesses**: Supraoptic, infundibular, pineal, suprapineal
- **Communication**: Foramen of Monro (to lateral ventricles)
- Volume: ~1-2 mL

**Cerebral Aqueduct** (Aqueduct of Sylvius):
- **Location**: Midbrain
- **Length**: ~15 mm
- **Diameter**: ~1-2 mm
- **Clinical**: Stenosis causes obstructive hydrocephalus

**Fourth Ventricle**:
- **Location**: Between pons/medulla (anterior) and cerebellum (posterior)
- **Roof**: Superior and inferior medullary vela
- **Floor**: Rhomboid fossa
- **Foramina**:
  - Foramina of Luschka (2, lateral): To cerebellopontine angle cisterns
  - Foramen of Magendie (1, median): To cisterna magna
- Volume: ~2-3 mL

### CSF Production and Flow

**Production**:
- **Site**: Choroid plexus (mainly lateral and fourth ventricles)
- **Rate**: ~20 mL/hour (~500 mL/day)
- **Total volume**: ~150 mL (50 mL ventricular, 100 mL subarachnoid)
- **Turnover**: 3-4 times per day

**Flow pathway**:
1. Lateral ventricles
2. Foramen of Monro → Third ventricle
3. Cerebral aqueduct → Fourth ventricle
4. Foramina of Luschka and Magendie → Subarachnoid space
5. Arachnoid granulations → Venous sinuses (reabsorption)

### Cisterns (Major Subarachnoid Spaces)

1. **Cisterna magna**: Between cerebellum and medulla
2. **Cerebellopontine angle cisterns**: House CN VII, VIII
3. **Prepontine cistern**: Anterior to pons, contains basilar artery
4. **Interpeduncular cistern**: Between cerebral peduncles
5. **Ambient cistern**: Lateral to midbrain
6. **Quadrigeminal plate cistern**: Superior to cerebellum
7. **Suprasellar cistern**: Above sella, contains circle of Willis

## Cerebellum

### Gross Anatomy

**Location**: Posterior fossa, inferior to tentorium cerebelli

**Size**: ~150 cm³, 10% of brain volume but 50% of neurons

**Divisions**:

1. **Lobes**:
   - **Anterior lobe**: Superior surface, motor control
   - **Posterior lobe**: Largest, motor coordination
   - **Flocculonodular lobe**: Vestibulocerebellum, balance

2. **Hemispheres and Vermis**:
   - **Vermis**: Midline structure
   - **Paravermis** (intermediate hemisphere)
   - **Lateral hemispheres**

### Functional Divisions

1. **Vestibulocerebellum** (Archicerebellum):
   - **Components**: Flocculonodular lobe
   - **Function**: Balance, eye movements
   - **Output**: Vestibular nuclei

2. **Spinocerebellum** (Paleocerebellum):
   - **Components**: Vermis and paravermis
   - **Function**: Posture, muscle tone, gait
   - **Output**: Fastigial and interposed nuclei

3. **Cerebrocerebellum** (Neocerebellum):
   - **Components**: Lateral hemispheres
   - **Function**: Motor planning, cognitive functions
   - **Output**: Dentate nucleus

### Deep Cerebellar Nuclei

1. **Dentate nucleus**: Largest, motor planning
2. **Interposed nuclei**: Emboliform and globose, motor execution
3. **Fastigial nucleus**: Most medial, balance and gait

### Cerebellar Peduncles

1. **Superior cerebellar peduncle**:
   - **Mainly efferent**: Dentate → red nucleus, thalamus (VL) → motor cortex
   - **Afferent**: Ventral spinocerebellar tract

2. **Middle cerebellar peduncle**:
   - **Afferent only**: Pontine nuclei → cerebellar cortex
   - **Largest peduncle**

3. **Inferior cerebellar peduncle**:
   - **Mainly afferent**: Vestibular nuclei, inferior olive, spinal cord
   - **Efferent**: To vestibular nuclei

### Cerebellar Cortex Layers

1. **Molecular layer** (outer): Parallel fibers, Purkinje dendrites
2. **Purkinje cell layer**: Single layer of Purkinje cells (output)
3. **Granular layer** (inner): Granule cells (most numerous neurons in brain)

### Functions

- **Motor coordination**: Timing and accuracy of movements
- **Motor learning**: Adapting movements through practice
- **Balance and posture**: Integration with vestibular system
- **Cognitive functions**: Language, executive function (lateral hemispheres)
- **Error correction**: Comparing intended vs. actual movements

### Clinical Correlations

**Lesion signs**:
- Ataxia (incoordination)
- Dysmetria (past-pointing)
- Intention tremor
- Dysdiadochokinesia (impaired rapid alternating movements)
- Dysarthria (scanning speech)
- Nystagmus
- Hypotonia

**Laterality**: Cerebellar lesions cause ipsilateral deficits (unlike cerebral)

## MRI Considerations for Anatomical Analysis

### Standard MRI Sequences for Anatomy

1. **T1-weighted**: Best gray-white matter contrast
2. **T2-weighted**: Good for overall anatomy, pathology
3. **FLAIR**: Suppresses CSF, good for periventricular lesions
4. **High-resolution 3D T1** (MPRAGE, BRAVO): Detailed anatomy, volumetrics

### Anatomical Variability

**Individual variation**:
- Sulcal patterns: Highly variable
- Arcuate fasciculus: Connectivity patterns differ
- Wernicke's area: Location varies
- Corpus callosum size: Sex and age differences
- Hippocampal size: Right-left asymmetry common

**Functional lateralization**:
- Language: 95% left dominant in right-handed, 70% in left-handed
- Spatial attention: Right hemisphere dominant
- Face recognition: Right hemisphere bias

### Age-Related Changes

**Development**:
- Myelination: Posterior to anterior, inferior to superior
- Gray matter: Peaks in adolescence, then decreases
- White matter: Increases through early adulthood

**Aging**:
- Cortical atrophy: Prefrontal > temporal > parietal
- Ventricular enlargement
- White matter hyperintensities (periventricular, deep)
- Hippocampal volume loss: ~1-2% per year after 60

### Coordinate Systems

**Talairach coordinates**: Based on AC-PC line and brain boundaries

**MNI coordinates** (Montreal Neurological Institute): More widely used, based on averaged brains

**Relationship**: Talairach and MNI differ; transformation formulas available

### Brain Parcellation Schemes

1. **Brodmann areas** (52 areas): Based on cytoarchitecture
2. **AAL (Automated Anatomical Labeling)**: 116 regions
3. **Desikan-Killiany**: FreeSurfer atlas, 68 cortical regions
4. **Destrieux**: FreeSurfer, 148 cortical regions
5. **Glasser MMP 1.0**: 180 areas per hemisphere, multimodal
6. **NextBrain**: 333 ROIs, ultra-high resolution histological atlas (2024)

## Clinical Applications

### Lesion Localization

Understanding anatomy enables:
- Predicting deficits from lesion location
- Localizing pathology based on symptoms
- Surgical planning
- Functional preservation

### Functional Mapping

**Pre-surgical fMRI**:
- Motor cortex localization
- Language dominance and localization
- Memory networks
- Visual field mapping

**Diffusion Tractography**:
- White matter tract identification
- Surgical trajectory planning
- Tumor infiltration assessment

### Normal Anatomical Variants

**Common variants**:
- Cavum septum pellucidum
- Cavum vergae
- Mega cisterna magna
- Asymmetric lateral ventricles
- Prominent perivascular spaces

**Significance**: Usually benign, must distinguish from pathology
