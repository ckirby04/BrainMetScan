# Brain Metastases - Clinical and Imaging Context

## Overview

Brain metastases are the most common intracranial tumors in adults, occurring when cancer cells spread from primary tumors elsewhere in the body to the brain. They are approximately 10 times more common than primary brain tumors.

## Epidemiology

- **Incidence**: 20-40% of all cancer patients develop brain metastases
- **Most common primary sources**:
  - Lung cancer (40-50%)
  - Breast cancer (15-25%)
  - Melanoma (5-20%)
  - Renal cell carcinoma (5-10%)
  - Colorectal cancer (5%)
- **Peak age**: 50-70 years
- **Distribution**: 80% supratentorial, 15% cerebellar, 5% brainstem

## Location Patterns

- **Cerebral hemispheres**: Most common (80%)
  - Gray-white matter junction (most frequent site)
  - Watershed zones between major arterial territories
- **Cerebellum**: 15%
- **Brainstem**: 5%
- **Multiple vs. Single**: 60-70% present as multiple lesions

## MRI Imaging Characteristics

### Signal Characteristics by Sequence

#### T1-Weighted Pre-Contrast
- **Signal**: Typically hypointense to isointense relative to gray matter
- **Use**: Baseline assessment before gadolinium administration
- **Key features**: Mass effect, edema (dark), hemorrhage (if present, can be bright)

#### T1-Weighted Post-Gadolinium (T1-Gd)
- **Signal**: Strong, often ring-like or nodular enhancement
- **Enhancement pattern**:
  - Nodular enhancement (solid metastases)
  - Ring enhancement (necrotic metastases)
  - Enhancement due to blood-brain barrier disruption
- **Key features**:
  - Well-circumscribed margins
  - Enhancement intensity often greater than primary gliomas
  - May show "tail" sign or dural enhancement

#### T2-Weighted and FLAIR
- **Lesion signal**: Hyperintense
- **Surrounding edema**:
  - Marked vasogenic edema (hyperintense on T2/FLAIR)
  - Often disproportionate to lesion size
  - Fingerlike pattern extending along white matter tracts
- **FLAIR advantage**: Suppresses CSF signal, better for periventricular lesions
- **Key features**: Mass effect, sulcal effacement, ventricular compression

#### T2* GRE/SWI (Susceptibility-Weighted Imaging)
- **Use**: Detecting hemorrhage and calcification
- **Hemorrhagic metastases**:
  - Melanoma (50% hemorrhagic)
  - Renal cell carcinoma (30-40%)
  - Thyroid cancer (30-40%)
  - Choriocarcinoma (highly hemorrhagic)
  - Lung cancer (10-15%)
- **Signal**: Blooming artifact (dark signal) indicates hemorrhage

#### Diffusion-Weighted Imaging (DWI)
- **Variable signal**: Depends on cellularity and necrosis
- **Solid portions**: May show restricted diffusion (bright on DWI, dark on ADC)
- **Necrotic portions**: Facilitated diffusion (dark on DWI, bright on ADC)
- **Use**: Distinguishing from abscess, assessing tumor cellularity

### Size Characteristics
- **Variable**: Range from <5mm to >5cm
- **Small metastases**: <1cm
- **Large metastases**: >3cm
- **Clinical significance**: Size affects treatment strategy (surgery, SRS, WBRT)

## Imaging Patterns and Features

### Classic Imaging Features
1. **Multiplicity**: Multiple lesions in different vascular territories
2. **Location**: Gray-white matter junction
3. **Surrounding edema**: Disproportionate vasogenic edema
4. **Enhancement**: Avid, often ring-like enhancement
5. **Well-circumscribed margins**: Sharp borders
6. **Mass effect**: Relative to lesion size

### Differential Diagnosis

#### Primary Brain Tumors
- **Glioblastoma**: Irregular thick ring enhancement, infiltrative margins
- **Primary CNS lymphoma**: Periventricular location, homogeneous enhancement
- **Meningioma**: Dural-based, broad dural attachment

#### Infectious
- **Abscess**: Restricted diffusion in cavity, smooth thin wall
- **Toxoplasmosis**: Multiple ring-enhancing lesions (in immunocompromised)

#### Vascular
- **Hemorrhagic stroke**: Follows vascular territory
- **Cavernoma**: "Popcorn" appearance, hemosiderin ring

#### Inflammatory/Demyelinating
- **Multiple sclerosis**: Periventricular, perpendicular to ventricles, incomplete ring
- **Tumefactive demyelination**: Open-ring sign, minimal mass effect

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms
- **Headache**: 40-50% of patients
- **Focal neurological deficits**: 20-40%
  - Motor weakness
  - Sensory changes
  - Visual field defects
- **Cognitive changes**: 30-40%
- **Seizures**: 15-20%
- **Increased intracranial pressure**: Nausea, vomiting, papilledema

### Presentation by Location
- **Frontal lobe**: Personality changes, motor deficits
- **Parietal lobe**: Sensory deficits, neglect
- **Temporal lobe**: Memory impairment, seizures, language difficulties
- **Occipital lobe**: Visual field deficits
- **Cerebellum**: Ataxia, coordination problems
- **Brainstem**: Cranial nerve palsies, altered consciousness

## Treatment Approaches

### Surgery
- **Indications**:
  - Single or limited metastases
  - Good performance status
  - Controlled systemic disease
  - Large symptomatic lesion
- **Goal**: Complete resection with minimal morbidity

### Stereotactic Radiosurgery (SRS)
- **Indications**:
  - 1-4 metastases
  - Lesions <3-4cm
  - Deep or eloquent location
- **Advantages**: Non-invasive, outpatient
- **Modalities**: Gamma Knife, CyberKnife, LINAC-based

### Whole Brain Radiation Therapy (WBRT)
- **Indications**:
  - Multiple metastases (>4-10)
  - Leptomeningeal disease
  - After surgical resection
- **Side effects**: Cognitive decline, fatigue

### Systemic Therapy
- **Chemotherapy**: Limited BBB penetration for most agents
- **Targeted therapy**: Based on primary tumor molecular profile
- **Immunotherapy**: Checkpoint inhibitors (pembrolizumab, nivolumab)

## Prognosis Factors

### Favorable Prognostic Factors
- Younger age (<65 years)
- Good performance status (KPS ≥70)
- Controlled primary tumor
- Single or limited metastases
- Absence of extracranial disease

### Graded Prognostic Assessment (GPA)
Scoring system incorporating:
- Age
- KPS (Karnofsky Performance Status)
- Number of brain metastases
- Presence of extracranial metastases
- Primary tumor type and molecular markers

## Follow-up Imaging

### Post-Treatment Response Assessment
- **Standard protocol**: MRI at 4-6 weeks, then every 2-3 months
- **Challenges**:
  - Treatment effect vs. tumor progression
  - Pseudoprogression (inflammation mimicking growth)
  - Radiation necrosis (can occur months to years post-treatment)

### RANO-BM Criteria (Response Assessment in Neuro-Oncology - Brain Metastases)
- **Complete Response**: Complete disappearance of all lesions
- **Partial Response**: ≥30% decrease in sum of target lesions
- **Stable Disease**: Neither PR nor PD criteria met
- **Progressive Disease**: ≥20% increase or new lesions

### Distinguishing Recurrence from Radiation Necrosis
- **MR Spectroscopy**: Elevated choline/creatine ratio suggests tumor
- **MR Perfusion**: Elevated rCBV suggests viable tumor
- **PET imaging**: FDG-PET or amino acid PET (FET, FDOPA)
- **Biopsy**: Gold standard when imaging equivocal

## Common Complications

### Neurological
- **Hemorrhage**: Particularly with melanoma, renal cell, thyroid
- **Seizures**: 15-20% of patients
- **Herniation**: From mass effect and edema

### Treatment-Related
- **Radiation necrosis**: Focal brain injury from radiation
- **Leukoencephalopathy**: White matter changes from WBRT
- **Cognitive decline**: Memory, executive function impairment
- **Pituitary dysfunction**: After WBRT

## Imaging Protocols

### Standard Brain Metastases Protocol
1. **T1-weighted pre-contrast**: Axial, sagittal (optional)
2. **T2-weighted**: Axial
3. **FLAIR**: Axial (essential for edema assessment)
4. **T1-weighted post-gadolinium**: Axial, coronal, sagittal
5. **DWI/ADC**: Axial
6. **T2* GRE or SWI**: Axial (hemorrhage detection)

### Advanced Sequences (Optional)
- **MR Spectroscopy**: Tumor characterization
- **MR Perfusion (DSC or ASL)**: CBV/CBF maps
- **DTI**: White matter tract assessment

### 3D Volumetric Sequences
- **3D T1 MPRAGE/BRAVO**: High-resolution pre and post-contrast
- **Advantages**:
  - Thin slices (1mm)
  - Multiplanar reconstruction
  - Better lesion detection
  - Volumetric analysis

## Key Points for Image Analysis

### Lesion Detection
- Small metastases best seen on post-contrast T1
- FLAIR useful for non-enhancing lesions
- Multiple lesions in different vascular territories
- Check gray-white junction systematically

### Lesion Characterization
- Size, location, number
- Enhancement pattern (nodular, ring)
- Surrounding edema extent
- Mass effect and midline shift
- Hemorrhage presence

### Treatment Planning
- Distance from eloquent cortex
- Relationship to white matter tracts
- Proximity to critical structures
- Surgical accessibility

### Response Assessment
- Lesion size (bidimensional or volumetric)
- Enhancement changes
- Edema changes
- New lesions
- Mass effect changes
